How the process usually works

  1. Apply for the grant before you start work. Starting early = ineligible.
  2. Inspection & approval. Staff verify your loss or proposed practice.
  3. Do the work & document it. Save receipts, invoices, seed tags, labor logs, and take before & after photos.
  4. Submit for reimbursement. Provide documentation; staff may re-inspect.
  5. Payment/reimbursement. Typically 30‚Äì120 days after paperwork is accepted; some practices can take 6‚Äì12 months.

AGI Limit Example (How the $900,000 Rule Works)

USDA (United States Department of Agriculture) programs have a general income cap: if your average income over 3 years is above $900,000, you may not qualify. There‚Äôs an exception if most of your income comes from farming, ranching, or forestry.

Example: A rancher earns $1.2 million over 3 years. $950,000 is from cattle sales and $250,000 is from non-farm investments. Since over 75% of their income is from ranching, they can still qualify by filing form CCC-942 at their FSA (Farm Service Agency) office. If most of their income had been non-farm, they would not be eligible.

Common Questions Farmers Ask

Can I get help if I lost hay or forage?

Yes. The Livestock Forage Disaster Program (LFP) can cover feed and grazing losses from drought or fire.

What if one of my cows died?

The Livestock Indemnity Program (LIP) provides direct payments if livestock are lost due to disaster, disease, or extreme weather.

Can I get a USDA grant for fencing?

Yes. Programs like ECP and EQIP often cover fencing repair, replacement, or cross-fencing. These are reimbursements ‚Äî apply first, then get paid back after work is done.

Do I have to pay upfront?

Most programs are reimbursements. Farmers often use USDA Microloans or Operating Loans to cover costs until payment comes through.
